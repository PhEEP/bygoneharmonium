02-12-13; Notes from a recent OCME Forensic Anthropology cold case review;

Memo in original case file states “attached list includes all outstanding missing persons in our files for the 1968 to present. It lists only males, 5’10” or taller.” The average estimated height on this case: 67.5 in (according to Dr. Hameli [former chief ME]) and 67.5-71 in (according to Dr. Angel). OCME ran a series of regression equations and found that if a range from about 67-73 inches is used, one can be 90% confident that the actual height falls within that range. More importantly, if Police only searched for individuals over 70 inches tall, they may have excluded many potential matches. This is especially the case given the statement made by Dr. Angel stating, “the stature estimate depends on race and lies between 67.5 and 71 inches, probably nearer the former.”

Skeletal remains present with mixed Caucasian and Black traits; may be Hispanic.

Dr. Angel’s reports revolves around his description of the pathological state of the remains. He notes a linear “scar” on the left temporal fossa. This may be an area of healing from a previous injury, the result of some condition, or simply a unique trait. Interestingly, Dr. Angel refers to bowing of the legs and a valgus deformity, indicative of “knock-knees.” The valgus deformity refers to an outward angulation of the distal segments of a bone. In the condition “genu valgum,” the tibia forms turned outward in relation to the femur, resulting in knock-knees. If this is the case, the person would most likely have walked with a limp or a shuffled gait, which would be noticeable to family, friends, etc.

Dr. Angel’s reports a “bruise” on the right tibia. Bruises don’t manifest on bones as they do on skin, but perhaps he is alluding to a possible effect of the knee condition.

Dr. Angel references an os acromiale, which occurs when one of the four ossification centers of the acromion process on the scapula fails to fuse. It is most commonly seen in Black individuals. It rarely causes pain but can sometimes be tender to the touch and impinge movement in the shoulder.

Dr. Angel states that the sacrum shows spina bifida. This is a birth defect commonly seen in the sacrum and lumbar vertebrae where an opening in the vertebrae exists, potentially allowing the spinal cord to slip out. There are several different types of spina bifida. “Occulta” is a condition where this is relatively hidden, possibly showing a dimple or depression in the lower back. It is sometimes associated with ankylosis, or immobility, in the lower back and appears to be more prevalent in Caucasians. However, the most common form of spina bifida is a more serious condition. It can protrude through the vertebral opening and be very noticeable as a bump on the back. It is associated with neurological disorders, could lead to paralysis, walking issues, and require assistance for moving around and dealing with bladder and bowel problems.

Dr. Angel’s description of the pathology may indicate that the spina bifida site is narrow, which would mean “occulta” would be the condition. However, the “knock-kneed” condition would still be present and likely alter mobility to some degree. If the serious case of spina bifida is present, then walking would certainly be an issue. Regardless of the type, the Spina Bifida Association states that 73% of those with spina bifida will develop an allergy to latex. This could be of importance to narrowing down missing persons. They also state that individuals with spina bifida can have lowered social skills, academic issues, and complications with the cerebellum including increased cranial fluid. In general, the totality of this information may be of value to finding a missing person and associating them.

As for the time since death estimation, since PMI estimation is less and less accurate the further away from the actual time of death one gets, the estimate should not be used to definitively exclude anyone missing less than 4 years before the discovery. It appears clear that the remains were there for a few years, given the level of postmortem alteration and weathering seen, but whether that means 2, 3, 4, 5 or more years since death is difficult to determine with precision.

The individual may have had malocclusion of the teeth as extensive wear was seen and noted.

Tooth #17 reflect wear as described in the dental examination and mentions possible orthognathia.

There are a few vertebrae and ribs unaccounted for, as Dr. Angel lists 5 missing vertebrae, while the ME found 18/24 vertebrae. Dr. Angel also lists 5 missing ribs, while the ME examined 17/24 ribs.
